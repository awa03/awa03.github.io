Chapter 7: Sedimentary Rocks

1. Be aware that, although rare in the crust as a whole, sediments and sedimentary rocks dominate solid Earth materials at the surface.

Answer

Sediments and sedimentary rocks are the most abundant materials at the Earth's surface. Sedimentary rocks are formed by the accumulation and lithification of sedimentary particles such as sand, clay, and organic matter. These particles are transported and deposited by water, wind, ice, or gravity, and then they undergo compaction and cementation to form sedimentary rocks. Sedimentary rocks cover approximately 75% of the Earth's surface, and they are important resources for oil, gas, and groundwater. They also provide a record of Earth's history and the evolution of life, as fossils are often found in sedimentary rocks. Despite their importance, sedimentary rocks are relatively rare in the Earth's crust as a whole, which is dominated by igneous and metamorphic rocks.

2. Be familiar with the sedimentary portion of the rock cycle, understanding physical and chemical weathering, erosion, transport, deposition, burial, and diagenesis.

Answer

The sedimentary portion of the rock cycle involves the transformation of rocks, sediments, and organic matter into sedimentary rocks through weathering, erosion, transport, deposition, burial, and diagenesis. Weathering breaks down rocks into smaller particles, erosion transports the particles, deposition settles the particles in a new location, burial occurs when sediment is buried under additional layers, and diagenesis transforms the sediment into solid rock through compaction and cementation. This process can repeat itself over millions of years.

3. Be able to distinguish among coarse-, medium- (sand), and fine-sized grains and to evaluate sorting, sphericity, and angularity among sediments and rocks.

Answer

Coarse-, medium- (sand), and fine-sized grains can be distinguished based on their size. Sorting refers to the similarity in size and shape of sediment grains within a sedimentary rock, sphericity refers to the roundness of the grains, and angularity refers to the sharpness of the grains.

4. Be aware of what distinguishes soil from loose sediment; the typical soil profile: O, A, B, and C horizons; and how these layers are distinguished.

Answer

Soil differs from loose sediment in that it contains organic matter and supports plant life. The typical soil profile includes O, A, B, and C horizons, which are distinguished based on the composition and physical properties of the soil layers.

5. Be aware of different types of sedimentary rocks (clastic, biochemical, organic, and chemical sedimentary rocks).
• Clastic sedimentary rocks are primarily classified on the basis of grain size and shape. Students should be able to recognize and characterize conglomerate, breccia, sandstone, siltstone, mudstone, and shale.
• Biologically catalyzed precipitation produces mineralized skeletons; these condense and lithify to form biochemical sedimentary rocks. Important examples are most limestones and bedded, biogenic cherts.
• Organic sedimentary rocks (the coal series) consist of the altered remains of trees and other plants that lived in boggy environments (which allow organic matter to accumulate without too much decay).
• Chemical sedimentary rocks arise from physically induced precipitation. As with biochemical rocks, classification is primarily on the basis of mineral content. Evaporites are an important subset of chemical sedimentary rocks and include rock salt (halite) and gypsum (small amounts of carbonate sediment can also form through evaporation). Chemical rocks can also form through the replacement of minerals during diagenesis; important replacement minerals include quartz (forming the rock called chert) and dolomite (forming dolostone).

Answer

Sedimentary rocks are classified into four types: clastic, biochemical, organic, and chemical sedimentary rocks. Clastic rocks are primarily classified based on grain size and shape. Biogenic and organic sedimentary rocks form from the accumulation and lithification of mineralized skeletons or organic matter. Chemical sedimentary rocks form through the precipitation of minerals.

6. Be aware of common sedimentary structures. The layering of rock is termed stratification (or bedding); cross beds signify deposition along a slope and can be used to indicate paleocurrent direction. Other features of interest within sedimentary rocks include fossils, ripple marks, and mud cracks.

Answer

Sedimentary structures include stratification (or bedding), cross beds (indicating paleocurrent direction), fossils, ripple marks, and mud cracks.

7. Be aware that the character of sedimentary rocks reflects their environments of deposition; be familiar with a few sedimentary environments (e.g., alluvial fan, fluvial, shallow marine, deep marine, glacial) and characteristics of the sediments deposited in these realms. Locally, environments change over time; one source of these changes is the sequence of transgressions (rising local relative sea level) and regressions (falling local relative sea level).

Answer

The characteristics of sedimentary rocks reflect their environments of deposition, and sedimentary environments can vary from alluvial fan to deep marine. Changes in sedimentary environments can occur due to rising and falling sea levels.

Chapter 7: Readings

Sediment consists of detritus (mineral grains and rock fragments derived from preexisting rock), mineral crystals that precipitate directly out of water, and shells (formed when organisms extract ions from water). Rocks at the surface of Earth undergo physical and chemical weathering. During physical weathering, intact rock breaks into pieces. During chemical weathering, rocks react with water and air to produce new minerals such as clay and ions in solution. The covering of loose rock fragments, sand, gravel, and soil at Earth’s surface is regolith. Soil differs from other types of regolith in that it has been changed by activities of organisms, by downward-percolation rainwater, and by the mixing of organic matter. Distinct horizons can be recognized in soil. The type of soil that forms depends on climate and source material. Geologists recognize four major classes of sedimentary rocks. Clastic (detrital) rocks form from cemented- together detritus (grains or rock fragments that were first produced by weathering, then were transported, deposited, and lithified). Biochemical rocks develop from the shells of organisms. Organic rocks consist of 2 altered plant debris or of altered plankton remains. Chemical rocks, such as evaporites, precipitate directly from water. Sedimentary structures include features such as bedding, cross bedding, graded bedding, ripple marks, and dunes. Their presence provides clues to depositional settings. Glaciers, mountain streams and fronts, sand dunes, lakes, rivers, deltas, beaches, shallow seas, and deep seas each accumulate a different sedimentary facies, a set of sedimentary rocks and structures. Thus, by studying sedimentary rocks, we can reconstruct the characteristics of past environments. Thick piles of sedimentary rocks accumulate in sedimentary basins, regions where the lithosphere sinks, creating a depression at Earth’s surface. Basins form where the lithosphere has been loaded by a weight or where it has undergone stretching. The sea level changes with time. Transgressions occur when the seal level rises and the coastline migrates inland. Regressions occur when the sea level falls and the coastline migrates seaward.

Chapter 7: Review Questions

1. How does physical weathering differ from chemical weathering?

Answer

Physical weathering is the breakdown of rocks into smaller pieces without any chemical changes in the composition of the rock, whereas chemical weathering involves the breakdown of rocks due to chemical reactions with substances such as water, oxygen, and acids.

2. Feldspars are among the most common minerals in igneous rocks, but they are relatively rare in sediments. Why are they more susceptible to weathering and what common sedimentary minerals are produced from weathered feldspar?

Answer

Feldspars are more susceptible to chemical weathering due to their chemical composition. They are weathered to produce clay minerals such as kaolinite, illite, and smectite.

3. What kind of minerals tends to weather more quickly?

Answer

Minerals that are more soluble or reactive tend to weather more quickly. For example, calcite and feldspars are more susceptible to weathering than quartz

4. Describe the different horizons in a typical soil profile.

Answer

A typical soil profile consists of four layers or horizons: O horizon (organic matter and leaf litter), A horizon (topsoil), B horizon (subsoil), and C horizon (parent material).

5. What factors determine the nature of soils in different regions?

Answer

The nature of soils in different regions is determined by factors such as climate, topography, geology, and vegetation.

6. Describe how a clastic sedimentary rock is formed from its unweathered parent rock.

Answer

Clastic sedimentary rocks are formed from the weathered products of pre-existing rocks. The weathered material is transported, deposited, and lithified to form a new rock.

7. Clastic and chemical sedimentary rocks are both made of material that has been transported. How are they different?

Answer

Clastic sedimentary rocks are made up of fragments of pre-existing rocks that have been transported and deposited, while chemical sedimentary rocks are formed from the precipitation of dissolved minerals from water.

8. Explain how biochemical sedimentary rocks form.

Answer

Biochemical sedimentary rocks form from the remains of living organisms that accumulate and are then lithified. Examples include limestone and coal.

9. Describe how grain size and shape, sorting, sphericity, and angularity change as sediments move downstream.

Answer

Grain size tends to decrease downstream, and sediment becomes more well-sorted and rounded due to abrasion during transport.

10. Describe how cross beds form. How can you read the current direction from cross beds?

Answer

Cross beds form when sediment is deposited at an angle to the horizontal surface, often due to current flow. The current direction can be determined by looking at the direction of the inclined layers.

11. Describe how a turbidity current forms and moves. How does it produce graded bedding?

Answer

A turbidity current is a sediment-laden current that flows downhill under the influence of gravity. It produces graded bedding because the heaviest sediment settles first and the finest sediment settles last.

12. Compare the deposits of an alluvial fan with those of a typical river environment and with those of a deep- marine deposit.

Answer

Alluvial fan deposits tend to be coarse-grained and poorly sorted, while river deposits tend to be better sorted and finer-grained. Deep marine deposits tend to be very fine-grained and well-sorted.

13. What happens during diagenesis, and how does diagenesis differ from metamorphism?

Answer

Diagenesis is the process by which sediment is lithified into sedimentary rock. It involves compaction, cementation, and other physical and chemical changes. Diagenesis differs from metamorphism in that it occurs at lower temperatures and pressures and does not involve recrystallization.

Chapter 8: Reading

Metamorphism refers to changes in a rock that result in the formation of a metamorphic mineral assemblage or a metamorphic foliation in response to change in temperature or pressure, to the application of differential stress, and to interaction with hydrothermal fluids. Metamorphism involves recrystallization, neocrystallization, phase changes, pressure solution, or plastic deformation. If hot-water solutions bring in or remove elements, we say that metasomatism has occurred. Metamorphic foliation can be defined either by preferred mineral orientation (aligned inequant crystals) or by compositional banding. Preferred mineral orientation develops where differential stress causes the squashing and shearing of a rock, so that its inequant grains align parallel with each other. Geologists separate metamorphic rocks into two classes, foliated rocks and nonfoliated rocks, depending on whether the rock contains foliation. Migmatite, a mixture of igneous and metamorphic rock, forms under conditions where partial melting begins. Rocks formed under relatively low temperatures are known as low-grade rocks, while those formed under elevated temperatures are known as high-grade rocks. Intermediate grade rocks develop under intermediate conditions. Different assemblages of minerals form at different grades. Thermal metamorphism (also called contact metamorphism) occurs in an aureole surrounding an igneous intrusion. Because there is no shearing involved, nonfoliated rocks form in contact aureoles. Dynamically metamorphosed rocks form along faults, where rocks are only sheared, under metamorphic conditions. Dynamothermal metamorphism (also called regional metamorphism) results when rocks are buried deeply during mountain building. Metamorphism occurs because of plate interactions: the process of mountain building in convergent zones causes dynamothermal metamorphism; shearing along plate boundaries causes dynamic metamorphism; and igneous plutons in rifts cause thermal metamorphism. The circulation of hot water causes a hydrothermal metamorphism (also called metasomatism) of oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridges. Unusual metamorphic rocks called blueschists form at the base of accretionary prisms. Metamorphism also results from meteorite impact and from convection in the mantle. We find extensive areas of metamorphic rocks in mountain ranges. Vast regions of continents known as shields expose ancient (Precambrian) metamorphic rocks. Metamorphic rocks return to the Earth’s surface due to exhumation. Exhumation occurs by erosion, by uplift and erosion, or by tectonic extension.

Chapter 8: Review Questions

1. How are metamorphic rocks different from igneous and sedimentary rocks?

Answer

Metamorphic rocks are formed from pre-existing rocks (igneous, sedimentary, or metamorphic) that have undergone changes in mineralogy, texture, and/or chemical composition due to heat, pressure, and/or fluids. Igneous rocks are formed from the solidification of magma or lava, while sedimentary rocks are formed from the accumulation of sediment and the subsequent processes of compaction and cementation.

2. What phenomena cause metamorphism?

Answer

Metamorphism is caused by heat, pressure, and differential stress. Heat can be added by burial depth or contact with hot rock/hot groundwater. Pressure is added with depth. Differential stress arises at fault

3. What is metamorphic foliation, and how does it form?

Answer

Metamorphic foliation is the development of a planar fabric in metamorphic rocks. It forms due to the alignment of inequant minerals (minerals with different dimensions) in response to directed pressure during metamorphism.

4. How is a slate different from a phyllite? How does a phyllite differ from a schist? How does a schist differ from a gneiss?

Answer

Slate, phyllite, schist, and gneiss are all members of the slate-phyllite-schist-gneiss series of metamorphic rocks, which form from the regional metamorphism of shale, mudstone, or siltstone. Slate has a fine-grained, dense texture and exhibits slaty cleavage. Phyllite has a shiny, micaceous appearance and a well-developed foliation. Schist has a coarse-grained, platy texture with a well-developed foliation, and typically contains mica, chlorite, and/or garnet. Gneiss has a banded appearance due to alternating layers of different mineral compositions, and typically contains quartz, feldspar, and mica.

5. How does prograde metamorphism differ from retrograde metamorphism?

Answer

Prograde metamorphism is the process by which a rock undergoes increasing metamorphic grade with increasing temperature and pressure. Retrograde metamorphism is the process by which a rock undergoes decreasing metamorphic grade due to a decrease in temperature and/or pressure.

6. Why are hornfels nonfoliated?

Answer

Hornfels are nonfoliated because they form in a relatively uniform, high-temperature and pressure environment, without the directed pressure required for foliation to develop.

7. Describe the geologic settings where thermal (contact), dynamic, and regional (dynamothermal) metamorphism take place.

Answer

Thermal (contact) metamorphism occurs when rocks are heated by the intrusion of magma. Dynamic metamorphism occurs in fault zones and shear zones, where rocks are subjected to intense deformation and pressure. Regional (dynamothermal) metamorphism occurs over broad regions during orogenesis, where rocks are subjected to high temperatures and pressures over long periods of time.

8. Why does metamorphism happen at the site of meteor impacts and along mid-ocean ridges?

Answer

Metamorphism occurs at the site of meteor impacts and along mid-ocean ridges because of the high temperatures and pressures generated by the impact or the spreading of the oceanic crust.

9. How does plate tectonics explain the peculiar combination of low-temperature but high-pressure minerals found in a blueschist?

Answer

Blueschist forms in subduction zones, where oceanic crust is subjected to high pressure and relatively low temperature. This combination of conditions results in the formation of minerals that would not normally be stable at low temperatures but are stabilized by high pressures.

10. Where would you go if you wanted to find exposed metamorphic rocks. How did such rocks return to the surface of the Earth after being at depth in the crust?

Answer

Exposed metamorphic rocks can be found in mountainous regions or areas with active tectonic activity. These rocks return to the surface of the Earth through uplift and erosion. Tectonic activity, such as mountain building and faulting, can cause rocks to be uplifted and exposed. Erosion then removes overlying rocks, exposing the metamorphic rocks at the surface.




Chapter 10: A Violent Pulse: Earthquakes

A fault is a fracture in the Earth’s crust along which movement has occurred. The movement can be vertical, horizontal, or a combination of both. The movement can be sudden, in which case an earthquake occurs, or slow, in which case the movement is called fault creep. Faults are classified by the direction of movement relative to the other side of the fault. A normal fault is a fault in which the hanging wall moves down relative to the footwall. A reverse fault is a fault in which the hanging wall moves up relative to the footwall. A thrust fault is a reverse fault with a dip angle of less than 45°. A strike-slip fault is a fault in which the movement is horizontal and parallel to the strike of the fault. A right-lateral strike-slip fault is a strike-slip fault in which the movement is to the right when viewed from either side of the fault. A left-lateral strike-slip fault is a strike-slip fault in which the movement is to the left when viewed from either side of the fault.
The theory of elastic rebound is the theory that rocks on either side of a fault are deformed by tectonic forces until their internal strength is exceeded. At this point, the rocks break and rebound to their original undeformed shape. The sudden movement of the rocks causes an earthquake.
Diffrent types of seismic waves include - P waves, S waves, and surface waves. P waves are compressional waves that travel through solids, liquids, and gases. S waves are shear waves that travel through solids. Surface waves are waves that travel along the surface of the Earth.
Earthquakes are measured through the use of seismographs. Seismographs record the vibrations caused by earthquakes. The vibrations are recorded on a seismogram. The amplitude of the vibrations is measured on the seismogram. The amplitude is the maximum distance that the seismograph moves from its resting position. The amplitude is used to calculate the magnitude of the earthquake. The magnitude is a measure of the amount of energy released by the earthquake. The Richter scale is a scale used to measure the magnitude of an earthquake. The Richter scale is logarithmic, meaning that each increase of one unit on the scale represents a tenfold increase in the amplitude of the vibrations. The Richter scale is limited in that it can only be used to measure the magnitude of small earthquakes that occur within 600 km of the seismograph. The moment magnitude scale is a scale used to measure the magnitude of an earthquake. The moment magnitude scale is not limited by distance or size.
Prediction of Earthquakes is difficult. Earthquakes are predicted by the use of foreshocks, changes in the elevation of the land surface, changes in the rate of flow of groundwater, and changes in the behavior of animals. Earthquakes are also predicted by the use of seismic gaps. Seismic gaps are areas along a fault where no earthquakes have occurred for a long period of time. Earthquakes are also predicted by the use of probability. The probability of an earthquake occurring in a given area is calculated based on the frequency of earthquakes in the area.
Tsunami's are caused by earthquakes that occur in the ocean. The sudden movement of the ocean floor causes the water above it to move. The movement of the water causes a tsunami. Tsunamis are characterized by a series of waves. The first wave is not always the largest. Tsunamis can be caused by earthquakes, landslides, and volcanic eruptions.

Chapter 8: Reading

Earthquakes are episodes of ground shaking, caused when earthquake waves reach the ground surface. Earthquake activity is called seismicity. Most earthquakes happen when rock breaks during faulting. A fault is a fracture on which sliding occurs. The place where rock breaks and earthquake energy is released is called the hypocenter (focus), and the point on the ground directly above the hypocenter is the epicenter. We can distinguish between normal, reverse, thrust, and strike-slip faults on the basis of the relative motion of rock across the fault. The amount of movement is called the displacement. Active faults are faults on which movement is currently taking place. Inactive faults ceased being active long ago but still can be recognized because of the displacement across them. Displacement on active faults that intersect the ground surface may yield a fault scarp. The intersection of the fault with the ground is the fault trace. During fault formation, rock elastically strains, then cracks. Eventually, the cracks link to form a through-going rupture on which sliding occurs. When this happens, the elastically strained rock breaks and vibrates, and this generates an earthquake. Faults exhibit stick-slip behavior in that they move in sudden increments. Earthquakes in the continental crust can only happen in the brittle, upper part of the crust. At depth, where rocks become ductile, earthquakes don’t occur. Earthquake energy travels in the form of seismic waves. Body waves, which pass through the interior of Earth, include P-waves (compressional waves) and S-waves (shear waves). Surface waves, which pass along the surface of Earth, include R-waves (Rayleigh waves) and L-waves (Love waves). Earthquake waves travel fastest through solid igneous rock, slower through sedimentary rock, slower yet through sediment, and slowest of all through liquids (S-waves will not pass through a liquid). We can detect earthquake waves using a seismograph. Seismograms demonstrate that different earthquake waves arrive at different times because they travel at different velocities. Using the difference between P-wave and S-wave arrival times, seismologists can pinpoint the epicenter location. The Mercalli intensity scale is based on documenting damage caused by an earthquake. Magnitude scales, such as the Richter scale, are based on measuring the amount of ground motion, as indicated by traces of waves on a seismogram. A magnitude 8 earthquake yields about 10 times as much ground motion as a magnitude 7 earthquake and 5 releases about 32 times as much energy. Most earthquakes occur in seismic belts, or zones, of which the majority lie along plate boundaries. Intraplate earthquakes happen in the interior of plates. Different kinds of earthquakes happen at different kinds of plate boundaries. Shallow-focus earthquakes associated with normal faults occur at divergent plate boundaries and in rifts. Earthquakes associated with thrust and reverse faulting occur at convergent and collisional boundaries. At convergent plate boundaries, we also observe intermediate- and deep-focus earthquakes, which define Wadati-Benioff zones. Shallow-focus strike-slip earthquakes occur along transform boundaries. Earthquake damage results from ground shaking (which can topple buildings), landslides (set loose by vibration), sediment liquefaction (the transformation of compacted clay into a muddy slurry), fire, and tsunamis (giant waves). Seismologists predict that earthquakes are more likely in seismic zones than elsewhere and can determine the recurrence interval (the average time between successive events) for great earthquakes. But it may never be possible to pinpoint the exact time and place at which an earthquake will take place. Earthquake hazards and casualties can be reduced with better construction practices and zoning and by knowing what to do during an earthquake.

Chapter 12: Deep Time: How Old Is The Earth?

1. Understand relative dating (e.g., cross-cutting relations, original horizontality ...) and absolute dating (e.g., isotopes, half-life...) methods. Most geologic periods were defined by relative dating (e.g., fossil succession).

Answer

Relative dating is a method of determining the age of rocks and fossils by comparing their positions in relation to each other. It includes principles such as superposition (the idea that younger rocks are on top of older rocks), original horizontality (the idea that sedimentary rocks are originally deposited horizontally), and cross-cutting relationships (the idea that a geological feature must be younger than any feature that it cuts across). Absolute dating, on the other hand, is a method of determining the actual numerical age of a rock or fossil. This can be done through techniques such as radiometric dating, which measures the decay of isotopes in rocks to determine their age based on their half-life.

2. Understand and be able to apply the principles that can be used to interpret the order of events that occurred to produce a given sequence of rocks.

Answer

The principles of relative dating can be used to interpret the order of events that produced a given sequence of rocks. By examining the positions of rocks in relation to each other, geologists can determine which rocks are older and which rocks are younger. For example, if a fault cuts across a layer of rock, the fault must be younger than the layer of rock it cuts across.

3. Fossil succession allows relative ages to be produced for many fossiliferous sedimentary rocks regardless of their spatial contexts. This allows for temporal correlation of widely separated strata on the basis of fossil content.

Answer

Fossil succession is the principle that fossils in sedimentary rocks occur in a definite and determinable order. This allows for relative ages to be determined for many fossiliferous sedimentary rocks, regardless of their spatial context. By comparing the fossils found in different rocks, geologists can determine the relative ages of those rocks and correlate them with rocks in other locations.

4. Become familiar with the geologic time scale: The ordered sequence of eons (Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, to Phanerozoic) and of eras within the Phanerozoic eon (Paleozoic, Mesozoic, to Cenozoic), as well as starting 6 dates for the eons and for the Mesozoic and Cenozoic eras.

Answer

The geologic time scale is a chronological representation of Earth's history. It is divided into eons, eras, periods, and epochs, based on changes in Earth's geology, climate, and life forms. The eons are the largest divisions of time and include the Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, and Phanerozoic eons. The Phanerozoic eon is further divided into three eras: the Paleozoic, Mesozoic, and Cenozoic.

5. Understand the distinction between relative time represented by the names of eras, periods, and so on, and numerical time represented by dates measured in years.

Answer

The names of eras, periods, and other units in the geologic time scale represent relative time. They indicate the order in which events occurred, but not the actual numerical ages of those events. Numerical time is represented by dates measured in years, and is determined through methods such as radiometric dating.

6. Understand that Earth is a geologically active planet and Earth materials are continually recycled so that the age of Earth is likely significantly older than the oldest rocks (~4 Ga). The widely cited 4.6 Ga age of Earth arises from radiometric dating of meteorites (orbital characteristics of the solar system imply contemporaneous formation of the Sun, planets, and asteroids)

Answer

Earth is a geologically active planet, and its materials are continually recycled. This means that the age of Earth is likely significantly older than the oldest rocks on its surface, which are approximately 4 billion years old. The widely cited age of 4.6 billion years for Earth comes from radiometric dating of meteorites, which are believed to have formed at the same time as the solar system.

Chapter 12: Readings

The concept of geologic time, the span of time since Earth’s formation, developed when early geologists suggested that Earth must be very old if geologic features formed by the same natural processes we see today. Relative age specifies whether one geologic feature is older or younger than another; numerical age provides the age of a geologic feature in years. Geologic principles (i.e., uniformitarianism, superposition, original horizontality, original continuity, cross- cutting relations, inclusions, baked contacts, and fossil succession) provide the basis for determining the relative ages of rocks and other geologic features. By working out relative ages, we can construct the geologic history of a region. The principle of fossil succession states that the assemblage of fossils in a sequence of strata changes from base to top. Once a fossil species goes extinct, it never reappears. Strata are not deposited continuously at a location. An interval of nondeposition or erosion is called an unconformity. Geologists recognize three kinds of unconformity: angular unconformity, nonconformity, and disconformity. A stratigraphic column shows the succession of formations in a region. A given succession of strata that can be traced over a fairly broad region is called a stratigraphic formation. The boundary surface between two formations is a contact. Several related formations in succession constitute a group. The process of determining the relationship between strata at one location and strata at another is called correlation. A geologic map shows the distribution of formations. A composite stratigraphic column that represents the entirety of geologic time is called the geologic column. The geologic column’s largest subdivisions, each of which represents a specific interval of time, are eons (Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, or Phanerozoic). Eons are further subdivided into eras, eras into periods, and periods into epochs. The numerical age of rocks can be determined by radiometric dating. This is because radioactive elements, found in small concentrations in many minerals, decay at a constant rate. During radioactive decay, parent isotopes transform into daughter isotopes. The decay rate for a given element is known as its half-life, the time it takes for half of the parent isotopes to decay. The ratio of parent to daughter isotopes in a mineral grain indicates the mineral’s age. The radiometric date of a mineral specifies the time at which the mineral cooled below a certain temperature. We can use radiometric dating to determine when an igneous rock solidified from a melt and when a metamorphic rock cooled from high temperatures. Other methods for dating materials include counting rings in trees, growth rings of shells, ice layers in glaciers... We can also study the sequence of magnetic reversals in strata. From the radiometric dating of meteorites and Moon rocks, geologists conclude that Earth formed about 4.56 billion years ago. Our species, Homo sapiens, has been around for about 0.000001% of geologic time.

    7. Contrast ice-house and greenhouse conditions.

    Answer

    Ice-house and greenhouse conditions refer to periods of the Earth's history when the climate was predominantly cold or warm, respectively. During ice-house conditions, glaciers and ice caps were more extensive, and during greenhouse conditions, there were high levels of carbon dioxide and warmer global temperatures.

    8. What are the possible causes of long-term climatic change?

    Answer

    Possible causes of long-term climatic change include changes in the Earth's orbit, variations in solar output, volcanic activity, the uplift of land, and the removal of CO2 from the atmosphere through the weathering of rocks.

    9. What factors explain short-term climatic changes?

    Answer

    Short-term climatic changes can be caused by fluctuations in solar radiation, changes in Earth's orbit and tilt, changes in reflectivity, changes in ocean currents, and catastrophic events such as volcanic eruptions and bolide impacts.
