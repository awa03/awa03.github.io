Metamorphic Rocks

Metamorphism

Metamorphic rock is formed from a preexisting rock or "proplith" at high temperature and pressure. Meatmorpism can occur to any proplith, but the resulting rock will depend on the composition of the proplith and the metamorphic conditions.

Solid State doesnt change?
- It does include weathering, diagenesis, or melting. - Metamorphic rocks often look totally unlike their protoliths.

Unique texture - Intergrown and interlocking grains.
Unique minerals - Some are only matamorphic
Unique foliation - A planar fabric from aligned minerals


Recrystallization - Minerals change size and shape without melting.
Phase change - Minerals change into a different mineral with the same composition.
Neocrystallization - New minerals grow from old ones.
Pressure solution - Minerals dissolve where they are in contact with water, and grow where they are not.
Plastic deformation - Minerals soften and deform without breaking.


Heat (Temperature)

Metamorphism occurs as the result of heat. Temperature ranges between 200c and 850c. Heat energy breaks and reforms atomic bonds. Heat is the most important factor in metamorphism.

Heat is generated by:
- Geothermal gradient (30c/km)
- Burial (10c/km)
- Intrusion of magma
- Friction
- Compression


Pressure

Pressure increases with depth within the crust. Temperature also increases with depth. Mineral stability is dependent on temperature and pressure. Pressure is important in metamorphism.

Differential stress
- Stress applied unequally in different directions.
- Results in foliation (planar fabric)
Tension - Pulls apart normal stress
Compression - Pushes together normal stress
Shear - Slides past shear stress


Compressional and shear can..
- transform equant grains into inequent grains
- cause inequent grains to allign.

Fluids

Hot water with dissolved ions and volatiles.
Hydrothermal fluids facilitate metamorphism.
- Speed up chemical reactions
- Facilitate ion migration

Causes of Metamorphism

The agents of metamorphism are:
- Heat
- Pressure
- Differential stress
- Fluids


Foliated metamorphic rocks - have a planar fabric.
- subjected to differential stress
- has significant component of platy minerals
- classified by composition and grain size


Nonfoliated metamorphic rocks - do not have a planar fabric.
- not subjected to differential stress
- classified by composition and grain size


Slate - very fine grained, low grade metamorphism, foliated, splits into thin sheets.
Has a "slaty clevage"
Phyllite - Fine mica-rich rock with foliation
- formed by low - medium grade alteration of slate.
- clay minerals neocrystallize into tiny micas.
Schist - medium to coarse grained, medium to high grade metamorphism, foliated, platy minerals.
- formed from phyllite at higher temperature & pressure.
Gneiss - medium to coarse grained, high grade metamorphism, foliated, banded.
- formed from schist at higher temperature & pressure.
Migmatite - medium to coarse grained, high grade metamorphism, foliated, banded, igneous intrusions.
- formed from gneiss at higher temperature & pressure.


Quartzite - very fine grained, high grade metamorphism, nonfoliated, quartz grains.
- formed from sandstone at high temperature & pressure.
Marble - very fine grained, high grade metamorphism, nonfoliated, calcite grains.
- formed from limestone at high temperature & pressure.


Hornfels - fine grained, high grade metamorphism, nonfoliated, clay minerals.
- formed from shale at high temperature & pressure.
Amphilbolite - medium to coarse grained, high grade metamorphism, nonfoliated, amphibole grains.
- formed from basalt at high temperature & pressure.


Metamorphic grade

Prograde metamorphism - increasing temperature and pressure.
Retrograde metamorphism - decreasing temperature and pressure.


Creates zoned bands of alteration in host rock.
= Called a contact (or metamorphic) aureole.
= Zoned from high (near pluton) to low grade (far from pluton).





1. How are metamorphic rocks different from igneous and sedimentary rocks?

Metamorphic rocks are different from igneous and sedimentary rocks because they are formed by the transformation of pre-existing rocks through heat, pressure, and/or chemical processes, rather than by solidification from molten rock or the accumulation and lithification of sediment.


2. What phenomena cause metamorphism?

Heat, pressure, and chemical activity cause metamorphism.


3. What is metamorphic foliation, and how does it form?

Metamorphic foliation is a parallel alignment of minerals in a metamorphic rock due to pressure. It forms when pressure is applied perpendicular to the direction of mineral growth.


4. How is a slate different from a phyllite? How does a phyllite differ from a schist?

Slate has a very fine-grained texture, while phyllite has a slightly coarser texture. Schist has a coarser texture than phyllite, and gneiss has the coarsest texture of the four.


5. How does prograde metamorphism differ from retrograde metamorphism?

Prograde metamorphism occurs when temperature and pressure increase, while retrograde metamorphism occurs when temperature and pressure decrease.


6. Why are hornfels nonfoliated?

Hornfels are nonfoliated because they form from contact metamorphism, which does not involve directed pressure


7. Describe the geologic settings where thermal (contact), dynamic, and regional (dynamothermal) metamorphism take place.

Thermal (contact) metamorphism occurs when rocks come into contact with a heat source, dynamic metamorphism occurs due to shearing and faulting, and regional (dynamothermal) metamorphism occurs due to large-scale tectonic processes.


8. Why does metamorphism happen at the site of meteor impacts and along mid-ocean ridges?

Metamorphism occurs at the site of meteor impacts and along mid-ocean ridges due to the high temperatures and pressures created by these events.


9. How does plate tectonics explain the peculiar combination of low-temperature but high-pressure minerals found in a blueschist?

Plate tectonics explains the combination of low-temperature but high-pressure minerals found in a blueschist because these rocks form in subduction zones where oceanic crust is pushed beneath continental crust, creating high pressure but low temperatures.


10. Where would you go if you wanted to find exposed metamorphic rocks. How did such rocks return to the surface of the Earth after being at depth in the crust?

Metamorphic rocks can be found in mountainous regions and areas of active tectonic activity. These rocks return to the surface through processes such as uplift and erosion.
