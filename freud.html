A Brief Introduction To Freud

Introduction

This essay centers around the ideas of Freud and how these ideas shape our desire, personality, etc. This is a generalized summary that contains everything you will need to have a basic understanding of Freud. I ask that if you struggle with any of the verbiages or may see something as repulsive (the classic sex with your mom for example) you first refer to the glossary at the end of the essay. I ask that you read this with an open mind because truthfully Freud is not as controversial as you may think, and much of the controversy centered around him is due to misinterpretations of his writings.

The Id

Let's begin with the Id. The id is driven by the pleasure principle and is the only developed system that is present at birth. The pleasure principle simply being the process of seeking immediate gratification. Take for instance food, the id, if you were hungry, would tell you to go grab food or get it to fulfill that desire. The id acts however solely on this desire for immediate gratification so it would urge you to say eat your roommate's food. Thus the Id is not subject to morality on its own. This explains why young children are pure proponents of desire not taking into account the needs of their mother (their object of desire, which will be later explained.) Freud summarizes this in the quote below (Intro To Psycho-Analysis, 14)

“It contains everything that is inherited, that is present at birth, that is laid down in the constitution-above all, therefore the instincts, which originate from the somatic organization and which find a first physical expression [in the ID] in forms unknown to us.”

So the Id can be briefly summarized as our human instincts, for desire. The Id is part of something Freud calls the Ucs, also known as the unconscious. The unconscious is one of 3; Ucs, Pcs (preconscious), and Cs (conscious). The unconscious is derived from the repression theory and is as Freud states a “Prototype of the unconscious” (The Ego & The Id, 5)

The Id bridges the separation between the body and the mind. It brings with it the instinctual desire for pleasure and finds a pattern within this. Seeking the activity that they desire most. It wishes to keep the nervous system stable, which masters the stimuli. The source of this is within the bodily organs.

The Id does not observe time (later further explained). This can be understood through the analogy of gambling or even playing video games. If we were to act on our desire and gamble time passes at a much more rapid rate because the id is not observing the time constraints you may have. This may also be a proponent of procrastination.

How do we Live with the Id (harmoniously)
To live with the Id we must first recognize its existence. The id as previously explained is amoral so we must strike boundaries with it, or negotiate (later explained). An example of negotiation would be having a cheat day once a week so that the id has a level of satisfaction but the principle of reality and —moderation is still met, without complete pleasure.

Resolution
However what if these desires cannot be met? Take for example libidinal desire, let's assume there is no way the individual can satisfy the needs of their libido. There is tension created because the desire cannot be met. These desires can be satisfied in other ways such as; dreams (mentioned later) or the visualization of that desire. The latter is known as the primary process. Primary Process examples are daydreaming, hallucinations, etc. So in the example given you may imagine having sex to curve your desire.

Oral Phase
The Id, as aforementioned, is the only developed system present at birth. This coincides with the oral phase. This is important because the Id makes sure all of the child's basic needs are met at birth, and shapes the later formation of the child. The oral phase is the child's phase of pure desire being met solely through their mouths. Examples are; a child is sad-> They cure this with their pacifier. So as Freud understands it is an issue within this stage (being deprived of the breast, etc) would lead to Oral Fixation within the individual. Take for example an eating disorder or a smoker, Freud would assume this issue was derived from a problem within the Oral stage.

Should we attempt to overcome the Id?
The simple answer is no. No matter how much we try the id is an essential function of the human psyche. Yoga as Freud describes is an attempt to overcome the id. Yoga is the attempt of overcoming instincts, through the complete control of instinctual life. This control of the instincts kills satisfaction itself to some degree. This is because the desires of the untamed ego are much more intense than the preserved ones of the tamed ego. Hence why genital sex is so desirable. It is an expression of the untamed ego's desire.

Practical Understanding Of Overcoming the Pleasure Principle (The Id)
Let's say you start a diet. This diet may be harder to follow if the diet is an immediate change. This is why ‘fad’ or ‘crash’ diets tend not to succeed. This is because the Id hates discomfort, and depriving the Id suddenly of satisfaction will cause it discomfort. This may also be applied to many other situations such as drug addiction. This is why quitting cold turkey may be impossible for some, the discomfort of the Id is too much to handle.

You will never be happy.
However, there is also the issue of the Pleasure Principle. The pleasure principle as forementioned is the “process of seeking immediate gratification.” However, this can never be fully fulfilled. Yet giving up on achieving the satisfaction of the pleasure principle is greater unpleasure than never succeeding in meeting the needs of the pleasure principle. We must come to some degree to the needs of the pleasure principle, and accept its existence. We must not avoid it, but recognize its importance of it, and impose the reality principle upon it (mentioned in The Ego).

The Ego

The ego is derived from the Id (created in the first 3 years of the child's life), through the changes in external stimuli. It envelops some of the Id but not all, it is then not sharply differentiated due to the lower half merging into the Id. The ego is only cut off from the Id by instances of repression. Therefore the ego acts as a mediary between the external and the internal world and allows communication between the ego and the Id. Even though the ego is a mediary it avoids some stimulus dulling down the external world. It portrays a shrunken-down version of what the ego is experiencing in the world around it. As seen in the quotes below (The Ego and The Id, 19)(Civilization and Its Discontents, 15)

“[…]The ego seeks to bring the influence of the external world to bear upon the Id and its tendencies, and endeavors to substitute the reality principle for the pleasure principle that reigns unrestrictedly in the Id.”
"Our present ego-feeling is, therefore, only a shrunken down residue of a much more inclusive — indeed, an all-embracing — feeling which corresponded to a more intimate bond between the ego and the world about it." The ego much like the Id seeks pleasure but it is more restrained in its manner. It does this by postponing internal satisfaction to meet the needs of the external world. It then seeks to maintain this pleasure through self-preservation. This imposition is known as the reality principle. As Freud puts it-

“It has the task of self preservation. As regards to external events , it preforms the task by becoming aware of the stimuli, by storing up exsperiences about them, by avoiding excessivly strong stimuli, by dealing with moderate stimuli, and finally by learing to bring about expedient changes in the external world to its own advantage. The ego represents what we know as common sense or reason, which is in contrast to the Id which represents passion. The ego strives to avoid anxiety; being object anxiety (external threat; starvation, punishment, etc) and neurotic anxiety (ego is overwhelmed by the id's desire, mainly sexual). This is due to not all desires being pleasurable. The ego seeks to min/max the desires of the id and superego. Take for example eating junk food on a diet. Your Id may feel satisfied because it tastes more appealing than the alternative however you may feel the pain as well of cheating on your diet. So the ego seeks to rationalize and negotiate between the two. The ego also works to transform the will of the Id as if it were it's own. This is due to as forementioned the ego being a product of the Id. Therefore the ego works within Cs, Pcs, and Ucs. The differentiating factor within the ego is language.

Freud describes the ego using the analogy of a man on horseback. This can be seen in the quote below (The Ego and the Id, 19). One might compare the relation of the ego to the id with that between a rider and his horse. The horse provides the locomotor energy, and the rider has the prerogative of determining the goal and of guiding the movements of his powerful mount towards it. But all too often in the relations between the ego and the id we find a picture of the less ideal situation in which the rider is obliged to guide his horse in the direction in which it itself wants to go.

The ego may appear to us as autonomous, acting as an entity. However, the ego continues inward past the points we may recognize (aforementioned). The ego defines its lines sharply, in relation to the object and the ego. The only threat to this separation between the object and ego however is love. This is a bonding of the object and the ego as one being. As Freud refers to it, “[…]’I’ and ‘you’ are one.” (Civilization and Its Discontents, 13). Within love the individual emotions may feel as though they are alien, this is due to the individual ascribing their emotions from the ego to the external world. From this we can determine the ego is not a constant but rather is dependent on other factors. This also warrants our theory that the ego evolves and is not constant from birth.

Ucs, Pcs, and Cs

Pcs
Preconciousness is not physically differentiated from the unconscious, because of this they are both physical. Something becomes preconscious through the connection with word presentations connected to it. The preconscious was at one point a perception, such as what you ate for dinner last night. These ideas can be drawn upon and recollected, or a better example maybe your birthday. The Pcs is also capable, for instance, you may awake from a daydream and solve a math equation within the dream. Thoughts within the Pcs are not repressed like the unconscious thoughts rather they are just latent. This however does not mean the Ucs coincide with the repressed rather, “it is still true that all that is repressed is Ucs but not all that is Ucs is repressed.” To add to this the Pcs are also a great deal closer to the Cs than the Ucs, even though it is only separated definitionally and not literally. To become Pcs something must establish a word presentation with it. Without word presentation, we have as Freud said, ‘a incomplete consciousness’. These word presentations may be distorted in different manners and intensities. The visual component to this process is then secondary. Take reading for example, without a mnemic to assign to the text there is no meaning in the words. The visualization is a product of the verbiage associated with it. This will later lead us to the importance of dreams.

“Cs is the surface level of the mental apparatus” (The Anatomy of the Mental Personality)(The Ego and The Id, 11)

Ucs
The unconscious is a product of the ego and Id and is a matter of perception. Simply put it is not being actively thought about. The Ucs motivate every action that we take (Take Freudian Slips). The Ucs does not observe time, and accidents do not occur from it. Every action according to Freud is purposeful. Take for example you accidentally hit your knee on a table, this may be a purposeful relief of pain and frustration within your life. The Ucs can also create disorganization of memory. Take for example a debate. Within this debate, if looking back on in say a month's time, the two debatings stories will not fully corroborate. One person may remember you saying something when the other may not. This can be due to intense emotions in the time period, examples being pain repressing some aspect of the memory. These extreme emotions can also lead to regression, which is a relapse in the psycho-sexual stages. In addition to this, the Ucs belong to the ego, however, the latent part (pcs) does not.

The mind acts similarly to the body of a human or animal. The comparison Freud provides is that of a developing child. The child may have the thymus glad replaced after puberty by the connective tissue. This however is still in a way preserved, as with the mind. Within the mind, instances may pass but can be reaccessed through regression. So the mind in a way grows and transforms from within itself, Freud provides the example of the marrow bone in this case. So some may be destroyed in the case of the connective tissue but also some may be transformed, to some extent.

The Ucs may also present guilt. This is due to the Ucs need for punishment and self-sabotage. I'm sure many of you can relate to this feeling of unconsciously ruining a relationship. The superego may also allow the aforementioned sublimation to occur.

We do not act in certain ways because they are stigmatized within society. Take for example a class setting, we do not blurt out an answer or thought unless prompted. This is due to the taboo associated with the setting. This in a broader sense, with more taboos is how our society operates according to Freud.

This starts with the primal horde theory. The basic thought behind this theory is that civilization and the rules surrounding it are based on a primal family. The family is the first sexual relationship and community. The father wishes to stay with his love object, the mother, and the mother wishes to stay with the children. As the children develop their lust for their mother grows. As understood within the Oedipus section. The Problem of Anxiety

The problem of anxiety is a more ‘niche’ book by Freud. I am going to briefly discuss the topics below in this section because it is such a dense book. I would not be surprised if this ends up being one of the longer text sections.

Freud defines two terms to begin, which shall be referenced throughout this entire next section. Starting with inhibition; inhibition is the restriction of a function, which does not necessitate pathology. It is a ‘normal’ reduction of a function. The next term is a symptom. A symptom is an indicator of a morbid process. It may also be a symptom. Freud explains the relationship between the two by speech. Speech causes us to speak of inhibition only when a reduction is present. So in this way inhibition and function are bound to one another. Freud states that this necessitates his investigation of the function of the ego and how it manifests through neurotic affection.

Inhibition is described through a few major points in men- The turning aside of the libido [psychic unpleasure], the absence of physical preparedness [non-erectibility], and the abbreviation of the act [ejaculatio praecox]. To put these in simpler terms the turning aside of the libido is unpleasurable sensations within sex or the inability to orgasm. Nonerectibility is the inability of a male to have an erection, and ejaculatio praecox is premature ejaculation. Freud however saw these as positive because they suspended the orgasm. Freud referred to this as cumulation.

Relationships and Anxiety
Relationships were cause for anxiety. Within sexual function, Freud stated that women dread it. He categorized this dread of sexual function as hysteria. Compulsive acts are another product of our sexual experience which is a product of precaution and assurance against sexual experience. They are therefore phobic.

Therefore the turning aside of the libido produced inhibition, which impairs the execution of the function. This renders it difficult through pure inhibition which prevents through cautionary measures. This is the discontinuance of the development of anxiety. This is a reaction or protest against the act and a desire to undo its cause. An example of this would be, as Freud gives, anorexia. Anorexia is caused by a withdrawal in the libido. Vomiting is a defense against the compulsion to eat. Therefore a refusal to eat is a product of anxiety.
